## The category, in one line

Konclave sits at the intersection of three visual categories that each carry a strong
pre-loaded aesthetic: **shielded crypto wallets** (Zcash), **treasury / multisig custody**
(Gnosis Safe, MPC), and **accounting / ledger tooling**. The strategic problem is that the
first two categories both default to the exact look the brief bans — "premium dark web3
dashboard." The third (ledger/accounting) is the visual whitespace, and it aligns with the
product's own promise ("transparent on the inside").

## What the personas visually expect (and how to subvert it)

- **Marina (non-technical treasurer, primary).** She does NOT come from crypto. Her mental
  reference for "money software" is a bank app or a spreadsheet, and her reference for
  "safe" is a physical object — a vault, a sealed envelope, a stamped document. A neon /
  glowing / gradient crypto dashboard reads to her as *risky, speculative, unserious*.
  The design must read as **institution and instrument**, not app. This is the single most
  important audience insight for the visual direction: the primary user is repelled, not
  attracted, by the category default.
- **Members (Alice / Bob / Carol).** They need to feel their approval is *weighty* and that
  "no one moves the money alone." Visual language of **quorum, seals, countersignature** —
  not a one-tap "confirm" button. State must always be legible.
- **The accountant (secondary).** Lives in rows, figures, and exports. Expects **monospace
  numerals, tabular alignment, printable/CSV artifacts** — the ledger aesthetic. This
  persona is the license to lean into archival/tabular visual language that the wallet
  competitors don't have.

## The whitespace, visually

`temp/05` establishes the *product* whitespace: local-first desktop + purpose-built
collective-vault + private-payroll, versus Zkool (power-user wallet), lamb356/frost-ui
(hosted web), Zashi (personal wallet), Gnosis Safe (transparent treasury). The **visual**
whitespace maps onto it precisely:

- Everyone shielded-and-collective (Zkool, frost-ui) looks like a **developer tool** —
  utilitarian, dense, unbranded.
- Everyone collective-and-polished (Gnosis Safe, MPC dashboards) looks like a **web3
  dashboard** — glass, glow, blue/violet gradients.
- **Nobody** in the field looks like a **sealed treasury instrument / archival ledger.**
  That is the open lane, and it is the lane the two ownable devices (tarja + mono-for-money)
  already point at.

## The narrative backdrop (visual restraint as a feature)

Post-NU6.2 (the Orchard soundness bug found in Jun 2026, fixed, no exploitation — see
`temp/05` §5), the audience's trust in "flashy crypto" is bruised. A **calm, sober, almost
bureaucratic** visual tone is not just on-brand — it is *timely*. Discretion reads as
maturity. Loud reads as the thing that just broke. The brief's "anti generic dark-SaaS"
instinct is also a market-timing instinct.

## The audience that actually votes

Judging is a **public ZecHub-DAO community vote** (`temp/05` §1, §6), and the video/README
drive votes disproportionately. This means the visual identity has a second job beyond the
app UI: it must **photograph and screenshot as distinctive** in a feed of dark crypto
projects. A generic blue dashboard is invisible in that feed; a redacted, sealed, mono
dossier is a scroll-stopper. The identity is a competitive asset for the *submission*, not
only the product.

## Takeaway for identity

The market does not need Konclave to be prettier than Zashi or more polished than Gnosis
Safe — it needs Konclave to be **categorically legible as a different kind of object**:
a sealed institutional instrument, not a crypto app. The differentiation is conceptual and
typographic (tarja + mono), and the palette's job is to *get out of the way of that* by
abandoning the crypto-blue signal. See `competitive-audit.md` for the positioning map.
